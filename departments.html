Church Departments

  2. Departments

The work of God at Buzuruga SDA church is divided into several departments and units to meet the various needs of God's people in our areas. The main goal of all these departments is to effectively fulfill the mission of the Adventists by helping everyone understand the Bible and find freedom, healing, and hope in Jesus Christ.

Departments at Buzuruga SDA Church

  • Church elder

    • The Church elder is a steadfast spiritual leader, a person with a good reputation for Christian character and conduct both within the church and in the community. They serve in the local church as an assistant to the pastor, who is appointed by the conference to serve the specific church. The elder supports the pastor in leading the church towards a deeper and more complete Christian faith through teaching, being an example, and overseeing programs that will help the church grow and fulfill its mission. When someone is chosen to be an elder of the church, the primary consideration is not their ability to preach or their position in the community, but rather their testimony of a holy life and their leadership abilities. The Elder of the Church is a leader.

  • Church Clerk

    • The Church Clerk is a vital part of the church's operations. Their duties include recording important church matters such as meeting minutes, official church letters, and maintaining various church records. One of the most important aspects within the church is the fellowship among its members, and one of the Church Clerk's tasks is to manage the church's membership records. One of the first responsibilities upon being appointed as Church Clerk is to ensure you have the church manual, preferably the latest edition. As the Clerk, you will need to understand how the church board operates because much of your work will involve close collaboration with the church board. The Clerk should carefully study and fully understand the sections of the Church Manual that cover membership and the procedures for council meetings.

  • Church Treasurer

    • Due to the important responsibilities of the treasurer, it is advisable to choose someone who can be re-elected to ensure continuity in the safekeeping and reporting of financial matters. Larger churches may appoint assistant treasurers according to their needs. The treasurer can greatly encourage faithfulness in returning tithes and foster a spirit of giving among the members. Guidance given in the spirit of the Lord will help members faithfully return their tithes and offerings to the Lord, even during financially challenging times.

  • Stewardship

    • The essence of the stewardship department's work is to encourage believers to make good use of what God has given them, including their abilities, talents, time, bodies, and even possessions. The local church, along with each individual member, is generally responsible for how they manage what they have been given, overseeing stewardship education and guiding people to understand stewardship in all aspects of human life. When we look at stewardship in its entirety, it is the foundation of mission and the goal of true discipleship. Many people often assume this department is solely focused on collecting money, but the truth is that the Stewardship Department exists to help believers become Christ’s disciples in managing wealth, talents, and all the blessings that God has bestowed upon humanity. Jesus spent much of His time speaking about possessions or wealth that people have. One-third of the parables He gave involved either wealth, money, or what a person possesses. This is because He knew that His listeners would not fully understand the principles of God's kingdom until they first understood their stewardship relationship with their God. In His Sermon on the Mount, He told the crowds, “For where your treasure is, there your heart will be also.” (Matthew 6:21). The Stewardship Department is very important for the spiritual growth of anyone who is serious about strengthening their relationship with God.

  • Deacon

    • When someone is chosen to be a deacon, it signifies that the church trusts that person because the Scriptures provide important guidelines regarding the character or conduct of the individual. If you are called to be a deacon, thank God, for the Bible promises that those who serve well as deacons will receive a good standing and great confidence in their faith in Christ Jesus. 1 Timothy 3:13 states, "For those who have served well as deacons obtain for themselves a good standing and great boldness in the faith which is in Christ Jesus."

  • Women’s Ministry Department

    • The Women’s Ministry Department is dedicated to encouraging, challenging, empowering, and nurturing Seventh-day Adventist women as they fulfill their role in advancing the gospel message worldwide. "The Lord has work for women as well as for men. Now they can take their place in this struggle, and He will work through them. ... They can perform work in families that men cannot do, work that reaches the depths of life. They can approach hearts that men cannot reach. Their work is needed." —Welfare Ministry, p. 145

    Vision
    The spiritual vision of the Women’s Ministry Department is to know Jesus with a whole heart, to love Him, serve Him, and trust Him.

    Mission of the Department
    The Women’s Ministry Department exists to guide, encourage, and challenge Seventh-day Adventist women in their journey of discipleship to Jesus Christ and as members of the global church. The mission of the Women’s Ministry Department, in a broader sense, is similar to the mission for all within the church—namely, to uplift Christ in the church and throughout the world.

  • Dorcas

    • Dorcas is not a department but a group. This group operates under the Personal Ministries Department. Dorcas focuses on outreach services, evangelism, and assistance for those in need with the aim of winning souls. The Dorcas group plays a crucial role in the church's mission activities; it is dedicated to identifying and addressing specific needs that impact individuals and the community at large, with the goal of opening doors for relationships and ultimately creating opportunities to preach the gospel. This group gathers and organizes clothing, food, and other necessities for the poor, needy, and distressed. The Dorcas group works in cooperation with male and female deacons and the AMO (Adventist Missionary Outreach).

  • AMO

    • AMO AMO stands for "Adventist Men Organization", which can be translated into Swahili as "Chama cha Wanaume Waadventista." Like Dorcas, AMO is not a department but a group operating under the Personal Ministries Department. It is an undeniable fact that for the church to grow rapidly and become strong, it is crucial to reach out to men. By reaching men, you effectively reach their families to a large extent. Recent research has shown that men can significantly impact their family's church attendance more than women and children can affect it. When a father attends church, the whole family usually attends as well. The AMO group aims to help men understand how to address their roles as Christian husbands and fathers, and to encourage their commitment and dedication to God's work. This group works in cooperation with male and female deacons as well as Dorcas.

  • Communicatin

    • Statistics from July 2015 indicated that the global population had already reached 7.3 billion. According to Statista, by January 2018, the number of internet users (one of the most modern tools in the communication industry) had reached 3.17 billion, nearly half of the world's population. This shows that a large number of people were already heavily engaged in receiving or sending information through modern communication methods. This means that over forty percent (40%) of the world's population were already using the internet. In 1995, the number of internet users was less than one percent (1%). The number of internet users increased tenfold from 1999 to 2013. The number reached one billion in 2005, two billion by 2010, and three billion by 2014. It is estimated that by 2020, more than half of the world's population will be internet users. This implies that as a church, we need to consider new ways to enhance our mission or evangelistic activities, and to communicate with people (with the aim of reaching them) through methods that enable us to reach a larger audience more quickly and efficiently. For example, according to Statista, by 2017, approximately 2.46 billion people were using social media to receive and share information worldwide. This is estimated to be more than one-third of the world’s population. Therefore, to reach people effectively according to current statistics, we should direct one-third of our evangelistic efforts towards using social media, as this is where many people are found. In the present era, the church must actively engage in mission activities using modern methods to reach people with the message of hope about what is coming to the world soon, namely the return of our Lord Jesus Christ. This is why the Communication Department encourages the use of a robust communication strategy and all modern communication techniques, sustainable technology, and media to spread the gospel —Church Manual [2010 Edition], p. 104.

  • Health

    • The church believes that the responsibility of making Christ known includes the moral duty of preserving human dignity by promoting high standards of physical, mental, and spiritual health. In addition to serving those who are ill, this responsibility extends to disease prevention through appropriate health education and leadership in encouraging good health without the use of tobacco, alcohol, other drugs, and unclean foods. When possible, members will be primarily encouraged to avoid eating meat.

  • Family

    • The purpose of family services is to strengthen marriages and families. The family was established by God’s creation, with marriage as its core. As the primary place where values are taught and the ability to relate to God and others is developed, the well-being of the family is crucial for the church’s mission to make disciples.

  • Publishing

    • The publishing services department is established to coordinate and support the promotion of book evangelism within the local church, under the management of the Publishing Services Council for the area. The department assists the church in developing sales and distribution of publications and other mission-related books by planning step-by-step strategies to engage church members in achieving these goals.

  • Religion Freedom

    • Worshiping God according to one's conscience and participating in the love of Christ is a valued freedom that is not enjoyed everywhere in the world. God's governance is built on the freedom to choose. God chose to create humans with the ability to choose as well. “See, I have set before you today life and good, death and evil... blessings and curses; therefore choose life, that both you and your descendants may live” (Deuteronomy 30:15, 19). Religious freedom is like a fragile flower; it must receive the strongest possible protection and the most careful nurture available. In this context, freedom of conscience can either thrive or be suppressed and weakened. Revelation 13 describes the political authority that will emerge in the last days and cause people to “worship” another religious authority with political power. Religion and politics are depicted in Revelation 17 as an apostate church supported by the state. Then, the force of persecution from this church-state union is symbolized by a woman who is drunk with the blood of the saints. What should we do about the threats and signs of the erosion of religious freedom? Ellen White said, “It is our duty to do all we can to prevent the impending danger. We should strive to remove prejudice by presenting ourselves in a proper light before people. We should bring before them the real issues involved, and thus intervene to prevent resistance that could be more harmful to measures protecting freedom of conscience” ~Testimonies for the Church, Vol. 5, p. 452.

  • Children

    • The Children’s Services Department coordinates all child-related activities in the church; it selects and trains teachers to instruct various subjects within the church; supports and assists child leaders; and advocates for the welfare of children. The Children’s Services Coordinator works with the pastor and the board to establish a Children's Services Committee to serve the children. This committee will consist of individuals who are passionate and experienced in serving children. Typical members include leaders of the Sabbath School divisions, the Vacation Bible School leader, Adventist Young People leaders, and two or three others with a passion for working with children. If the church has a Children’s Services Department, Vacation Bible Schools, children’s Sabbath School branches, neighborhood Bible clubs, and story hours will fall under the leadership of the Children’s Services.

  • Listening

    • It is crucial that those passionate about gospel listening due to the church's mission efforts be attended to promptly. Church members often build relationships with non-members through evangelistic meetings, various seminars, attending services with guests, and other events that offer opportunities to meet and build connections. Similarly, there are individuals within the church who, through their appeal and friendship with those around the church, develop relationships that lead others to be attracted to and appreciate the church or its services. In all of this, it is the responsibility of the listening leader to create an environment that maintains relationships and encourages individuals to join the church, while also establishing ways to follow up with those who are interested in the church's services.

  • Voice of Prophecy

    • The Voice of Prophecy exists to proclaim the everlasting gospel of Christ—leading people to accept Jesus as their personal Savior and nurturing them to prepare for His soon return. The Voice of Prophecy has been leading in faith-based broadcasting for over 90 years. Founded in 1929 by H.M.S. Richards Sr., the ministry focuses on providing Christ-centered media that highlights God’s love, announces His imminent return, offers hope to the brokenhearted, and provides guidance for daily living. Supporting evangelism for the local church is the core of the Voice of Prophecy's mission. Hundreds of churches across North America use Voice of Prophecy media and evangelistic resources to reach their communities for Christ in relevant and effective ways. Its long-established Bible study materials are available in over 70 languages and in various user-friendly formats, both printed and digital. Additionally, its humanitarian projects in countries like India and Myanmar address both physical and spiritual needs. The Voice of Prophecy offers many valuable teachings suitable for both baptized members and those preparing for baptism.

  • Music

    • "Music was given to fulfill a holy purpose, to lift thoughts toward that which is pure, noble, and elevating, and to inspire worship and thanksgiving to God." — PP 594. "Jesus communicated with heaven through songs." — DA 73. Music is one of the highest forms of art. Good music not only brings us joy but also elevates our minds and enhances our finest qualities. Frequently, God has used spiritual songs to touch the hearts of sinners and lead them to repentance. On the other hand, music can also corrupt morals and disrupt our relationship with God. We must be very careful in choosing music for our homes, social gatherings, schools, and churches. Any music, whether jazz, rock, or any related mixed genres, or lyrics expressing trivial or unimportant emotions, should be avoided.

  • Youth

    • Youth are not only the Church of tomorrow but also the Church of today. The significance of youth continues to be evident daily, not just in the Church but also in the community. The Adventist Church is also a church of this generation's youth. Youth are powerful individuals with great potential for thought and action in their lives; hence the Scriptures say: "I write to you, young men, because you are strong, and the word of God abides in you, and you have overcome the wicked one." —1 John 2:14 Regarding youth, the prophet says: "For the work to advance in all its areas, God needs the strength, zeal, and courage of youth. He has chosen young people to help advance His work. Wise planning and bold action require fresh, unyielding strength. Young men and women are invited to offer their youthful energy to God, so that using their strength, thoughtful minds, and courage, they may bring glory to God and salvation to their fellow humans." —Gospel Workers, p. 67

    The Church is Also for Youth
    Historically, the Seventh-day Adventist Church did not have a specific youth department; youth were involved in church activities just like any other members. However, it's important to remember that the revival movement of the 1840s was largely driven by young people. This movement was led by individuals like James White, who began preaching at age 21, Ellen White, who received her first vision at age 17, and John Loughborough, who started preaching at age 17. These young people did not need someone to minister to them; they were the ministers themselves, engaging passionately with the truths they discovered in the Word of God.

    Origins of the Youth Department
    The truth is that the Youth Department was established by two young individuals who saw the need for it and brought it to prayer. One young man, Luther Warren, was 14 years old at the time, and the other, Harry Fenner, was 17. In 1879, they knelt under a tree in their hometown of Hazelton, Michigan, praying for guidance on how to reach other young people and bring them to Jesus. Guided by their prayers, these two young people established the first youth missionary band of the church, consisting of nine boys. The Hazelton youth band, which included prayer, hymn singing, officer elections, reports on mission work, and contributions for publishing, quickly grew into a youth community that included both boys and girls. Their activities not only involved religious services but also social events popular at the time. Although parents and other adults in the church were involved, leadership remained primarily in the hands of the youth, especially Luther Warren, who continued his youth ministry and evangelistic work within the Seventh-day Adventist Church throughout his life. Later, a similar youth missionary band was established in 1891 in Antigo, Wisconsin, initiated by a young man named Meade MacGuire. Within a few years of the establishment of the first youth group, many other similar groups were formed in Seventh-day Adventist churches across the United States. The first such community outside North America was organized in Adelaide, Australia, in 1892. In the General Conference session of 1901, the Seventh-day Adventist Church took its first steps toward organizing its youth ministry. A proposal was passed to arrange this youth community and create a committee to oversee this process. Luther Warren, then a prominent young figure in the Seventh-day Adventist Church, was chosen as the chairman of this committee. For the first time, the Youth Department began to gain recognition in the Church. At that time, the church had placed youth under the Sabbath School Department, and Mrs. L. Flora Plummer (one of the Sabbath School leaders) was assigned as the youth mentor. By 1905, the Adventist youth work was growing not only in the United States but also in Australia, Germany, England, Trinidad, Jamaica, Canada, and other countries.
    By 1907, the Youth Department continued to grow and reached Africa, Tahiti, Singapore, Fiji, Portugal, Bermuda, Japan, the Philippines, and Central America. In 1907, the Youth Department was known as the Seventh-day Adventist Young People's Department and was popularly referred to as MV, which stands for Missionary Volunteers. In 1978, the name changed to Adventist Youth (AY). In 2005, the department's name was changed to Youth Ministries Department, encompassing three levels of youth ministry: Explorers, Pathfinders, and Adventurers. In 2015, the name changed again to Adventist Youth Ministries.

  • Sabbath School

    • Essentially, the Sabbath School, which is a core educational program in the Church, has four main goals: studying the Scriptures, fellowship, community service, and emphasizing world mission. The Sabbath School and Personal Ministries Department of the General Conference distributes Bible study guidelines for all age levels, provides program structures for Sabbath Schools in the context of various cultural divisions worldwide, offers guidance and training systems for Sabbath School teachers, and encourages the giving of Sabbath School offerings for world mission. “Sabbath School is a vital branch of missionary work, not only because it provides knowledge of the Word of God to both youth and adults, but because it instills in them a love for the sacred truths of that Word and a desire to study it themselves; above all, it teaches them to conduct their lives in harmony with the sacred teachings of that truth.” —CSW 10, 11
